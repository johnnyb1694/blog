The functional nature of Chinese

2019-04-22

Seize the day!

One of my all-time favourite scenes in popular cinema comes from Richard Linklater’s film “Boyhood”:

In it, the protagonist (Mason) concedes that he is constantly ‘seized’ by the moment, rather than the ‘moment’ being seized by him and I often feel the same way about my life. So many things have happened to me that weren’t pre-conceived on my part. ‘Carpe diem’ is a lot more like, ‘Carpe…wait, what?’ when it comes to my life.

Indeed, when I am asked (regularly) why I chose to learn Chinese, my answer, as cliche as it sounds, is that Chinese probably chose me and I ran with it - much the same way in which the moment seized the character Mason. There was no grand narrative or reasoning behind my choice.

Chinese is more simple than you think…

The thing about Chinese that makes it different to other languages I’ve tried to (had to!) learn in the past - such as French and German - is that the creators of the language had obviously paid close attention to design from the outset (that or they got lucky, which is also possible).

Let’s start with the concept of joining ‘characters’ as opposed to joining items of an ‘alphabet’: this is one aspect of Chinese or Japanese which often strikes fear into the hearts of those interested in learning these languages. People are used to having a finite alphabet. By contrast, they probably don’t like the uncertainty associated with the seemingly infinite number of Chinese characters.

However, this fear is misplaced.
